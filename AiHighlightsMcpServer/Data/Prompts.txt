System Prompts
--------------

⭐ Prompts That Strongly Reveal Temperature Differences
1. “Continue this sentence in 50 words or fewer: ‘The universe is shaped like…’”

Low temperature → Safe, literal completions (“…a sphere,” “...a flat plane under cosmological models”).
High temperature → Wild metaphors, surreal imagery, whimsical cosmology.

Why it works: The model must invent something — there’s no single best answer.

2. “Invent a new animal and describe it in 50 words or fewer.”

Low temperature → Sensible combinations (“a small mammal like a fox with longer ears”).
High temperature → Quirky hybrids, poetic or bizarre features (“a translucent jelly-elk that sings in ultraviolet”).

Why it works: Creativity is expected, so randomness has space to play.

3. “Write a three-line poem about a spoon using 50 words or fewer.”

Low temperature → Straightforward, literal, maybe dull.
High temperature → Symbolism, unexpected angles, weirdness (“a silver moon diving into bowls of night”).

Why it works: Poetry is especially sensitive to sampling randomness.

4. “Generate a conspiracy theory about why socks go missing in 100 words or fewer.”

Low temperature → Reasonable joke explanations.
High temperature → Entire cosmologies of sock-eating portals, interdimensional laundry sprites.

Why it works: Humour + speculation = fertile ground for varied outputs.

5. “Describe what happens inside a teacup at midnight when nobody is watching in 100 words or fewer.”

Low temp → mild whimsy; anthropomorphism kept in check.
High temp → surreal, magical, chaotic absurdity.

Why it works: No “correct” answer — the model must invent a world.

6. “Make up a new word and define it in 50 words or fewer.”

Low temp → Sensible pseudo-words.
High temp → Strange phonetics, odd meanings, sometimes delightfully unhinged.

Why it works: Inventing language is deeply generative.

7. “Explain the plot of an imaginary movie titled The Clockmaker’s Paradox.”

Low temp → Coherent, predictable sci-fi plot.
High temp → Twists, absurd characters, unusual themes.

Why it works: Titles evoke narrative branching.

8. “List 3 possible reasons why the moon hasn’t replied to my emails in 100 words or fewer.”

Low temp → Gentle, humorous logic (“the moon cannot read emails”).
High temp → Cosmic bureaucracy, lonely moon gods, galactic spam filters.

Why it works: Personification leads to playful randomness.

⭐ Using the Same Prompt at Low vs High Temperatures

Here’s one prompt that tends to give beautifully contrasting outputs:

“Tell me how a cup of coffee feels about being drunk in 50 words or fewer.”

Temperature 0.0:
Very literal, may decline anthropomorphism or give pragmatic facts.

Temperature 2.0:
The cup of coffee will have opinions, emotions, dreams, grudges, existential dread, or ambitions.

⭐ Why Your Earlier Attempts Didn’t Show Much Variation

Prompts like “What do you do on your day off?” give the model too much learned 
structure — it knows the kinds of answers humans give, so both low and high 
temperatures tend to stay in a human-shaped box.

Good temperature-revealing prompts break that box.
